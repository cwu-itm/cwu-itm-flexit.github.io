Module 1.1 Blog Post

Tuning My Mindset for Digital Design

As I warily begin this digital design course, I am realizing that building modern digital environments requires a fairly different mindset than what I used in most of my earlier classes. With some work it is easy to focus on just one thing at a time, like writing a research paper, an isolated piece of code, or designing a mostly static web page layout. But real-world digital spaces, like online banking apps or cloud networks, do not really work in isolation. They are made of lots and lots of moving parts that all talk to each other at the same time, and the design and production pipelines should ideally be talking to each other early too. If I try to design these environments by looking only at small, separated pieces, I will be more likely to miss how they affect one another. To build digital tools that actually last and stay safe I could use systems thinking and bring it to life with a model-based workflow. This post is my reflection on what it means to think in systems, how it stretches beyond my traditional linear habits, and why using a shared model may be the best way to handle heavy digital complexity.

What Systems Thinking Means to Me

To try to be straightforward, systems thinking to me just means choosing to look at the relationships between things rather than just the things themselves. Instead of staring at one single part, I could step back and look at how that part connects to and influences everything around it, and also the user experience as a whole. It is the idea that you cannot really understand how a whole system behaves if you only look at its pieces in a vacuum. After all, a pile of bicycle parts sitting on the floor cannot get you anywhere; it only works when the parts are connected so that energy can flow from the pedals to the wheels (and when you know how to ride a bike).

This perspective goes back to Ludwig von Bertalanffy’s general systems theory in the 1960s (Bertalanffy, 1968). As our textbook Systems Engineering Demystified explains, this theory is a conceptual framework based on the principle that the component parts of a system are best understood in the context of their relationships with each other and with other systems, rather than in isolation. For me as a newbie designer, this means elements are never really by themselves; they always exist in relation to other parts.

To make this a bit more practical, I can use Peter Checkland's five system categories: natural systems (like the weather), designed physical systems (like smartphones and servers), designed abstract systems (like algorithms and conceptual models), human activity systems (like groups of people working together), and transcendental systems (things beyond our current understanding) (Checkland, 1999). A modern digital environment almost never fits into just one of these buckets. Instead, it blends them together.

If I think about a ride-sharing or food-delivery app (which I painfully used to work) it uses physical hardware like phones and servers. It relies on fairly abstract systems like software code and integrated maps. It connects to a human activity system of drivers and passengers. It even reacts to construction, traffic, or natural weather changes. By using systems thinking, I can separate my main focus, the "System of Interest" that I am building, from the "Enabling Systems" around it, like cellular networks and payment systems. This keeps me from designing software in a bubble while forgetting the real world it has to live in.

Moving Past Linear Thinking Traditionalism

To see why this matters, I have to contrast it with traditional, linear thinking. Linear thinking views the world as a straight line of cause and effect: action A causes result B, and that is generally it. It assumes you can understand any complex situation just by breaking it down into tiny pieces, studying each piece separately, and adding them back together. While that works for some basic mechanical fixes, it seems to fall apart in digital design. Digital spaces are full of loops where an action in one corner ripples out, changes things elsewhere, and then comes back to affect where it started.

When teams stick to linear thinking, they build organizational silos. The database team only worries about storage, the frontend team only looks at visuals, and the security team only watches network ports. Because everyone works in isolation, they are more blind to how their choices might mess with each other (Bento et al., 2020).

For example, if a school enrollment/registration website slows down, a linear thinker might just say, "The database is overloaded, so let's cache the data to lighten the load." But if they do this without looking at the whole system, it could cause an unexpected loop. The cached data might stop the frontend from showing real-life seat openings. Students then see open seats that are actually full, get frustrated, and start spamming the refresh button. This sudden rush of traffic can crash the login servers on a separate part of the site. The linear fix accidentally makes things way worse. Systems thinking teaches us to look for "emergent properties"—behaviors that only show up when all the parts are running together, which you can never find by looking at a single component alone. Shifting my focus to relationships helps me design software that can handle these unexpected waves.

The Value of a Model-Based Approach

Once I accept that I need to think in systems, the practical challenge is managing all these connections during a project. As designs get complicated, just winging it does not work (though we wish it did). In the past, teams used a document-based approach, scattering information across hundreds of text files, spreadsheets, and slideshows. As Chapter 2 of Systems Engineering Demystified points out, this old way makes it incredibly hard to deal with modern, connected systems. Our textbook notes that major project failures usually come down to three evils: unmanaged complexity, broken or confusing communication, and a lack of understanding when people ignore different viewpoints and make lazy assumptions. In a document-heavy project, knowledge is trapped in separate files. If a requirement changes, someone has to find and fix every single mention of it by hand, which leaves the door wide open for mistakes and outdated plans.

Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) fixes this by changing where our project knowledge lives. Instead of using scattered documents, we put all our system data into one centralized blueprint model that simplifies the system. The goal is not to map out every single atom of the universe, but just to capture enough useful information to successfully build the project.

From this single source of truth, we can pull out different tailored "Views" for different people. A network tech can look at a view of the hardware, a developer can look at a view of the data tables, and a manager can watch the requirements list. Because every single view updates dynamically from the exact same central database, they have a better chance at staying consistent. If I change a requirement, it updates everywhere instantly, killing the risk of teams working from conflicting scripts.

This model-centric approach is supported by Salayandía et al. (2006), who looked at how science teams and computer scientists struggle to build complex workflows together because they use different terms and focus on different levels of detail. To fix this, the researchers used a Model-Based Workflow (MBW) built on a shared project dictionary, or ontology, designed by the domain experts. By using this centralized model, they helped remove the confusing technical properties that usually make system plans look too complicated for outside stakeholders to understand (and me frankly). This shared model gave the teams a clear framework to build their systems with minimal back-and-forth, proving that moving away from the ol’ messy documents structure to a centralized model makes it much easier for different tech teams to communicate and collaborate without getting too lost in translation.

According to our text, a solid model-based framework relies on four basic building blocks to stay organized: an ontology (the project dictionary that keeps terms consistent), a viewpoint layer (the blueprint showing what each stakeholder needs), a notation (a visual language like SysML to draw clear diagrams), and a process set (the step-by-step rules for building and checking our views over time).

A good model always balances two sides of a system: its structure (the anatomy of what the elements are, where the boundaries sit, and how parts connect) and its behavior (the physiology of what order things happen in and how components react to triggers). Mapping both structure and behavior in one model lets me see exactly where the trickiest parts of the system live so I can fix them before writing code.

Last Thoughts

Reflecting on the first materials for the course, I am learning that mastering tools like heavy HTML layouts or custom scripts is only a small piece of being a good designer. I also doubt I will truly ever master those anyways. However, the real value is the mindset I use to organize those tools. If I stick to a narrow, linear view, I will end up building fragmented software that breaks easily. Systems thinking changes that by making me focus on the connections between code, databases, various types of human users, and outside systems. Combining this holistic view with a shared model gives me a practical way to keep future projects more aligned, clear, and ready for real-world complexity.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

Breaking Down Systems and Interfaces

Moving further into my exploration of digital systems design, I am realizing that just understanding a system's internal code or products alone is not enough to build even a decent project. As our textbook, Systems Engineering Demystified (Holt, 2023), points out in Chapter 3, the real groundwork of any system model comes down to the individual elements making up the organizational hierarchy and the shared connections, or interfaces, between them. To avoid building digital tools in a little development bubble, I should try to look closely at how these elements connect, where their boundaries sit, and how they communicate both sideways and up or down too.

According to Holt (2023), working with Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) requires a shared project dictionary, known as an ontology (not an ornithology, that’s the study of birds), to try to make sure that all of our diagrams and model views match up perfectly and stay entirely consistent. We can see this structure clearly when expanding a simple system hierarchy. A basic model includes the everyday stakeholders who hold an interest in the system, which in turn breaks down into smaller subsystems. To make our models flexible yet precise, we use different types of modeling relationships to connect these parts. A relationship where one part completely owns another is shown using a composition connection, while an optional, non-owned relationship uses an aggregation connection. These are terms I know from Object-Oriented Programming where the main difference lies in the strength of the ownership and the lifecycle dependency between the container and the part. For example, if I am looking at a vehicle system view, the chassis, the core drivetrain, or the alternator would all be owned subsystems because a modern car cannot exist without them. On the other hand, an optional accessory like a sticker decal or a bike rack represents what's called an aggregation because it is extra. Although, I guess bikers may not find that to be a negotiable add-on.

As the projects get larger and more complicated, this structural hierarchy could naturally expand to include lower levels of detail. Basically, systems comprise subsystems, subsystems comprise assemblies, and assemblies break down into individual components (have you ever played the game Factorio?). Moving the individual parts through these systems and subsystems in the proper order becomes important here. As John Boardman puts it in his book Systemic Thinking: Building Maps for Worlds of Systems, “to avoid systemic failure is to understand systems as parts, and to do that we need systemic thinking (Boardman, 2013, p. 10).” To keep our system models organized and easy to follow, the project dictionary sets up clear rules that forbid us from taking messy shortcuts or jumping lines when handling each part. This means that a large, high-level system cannot just skip over the middle layers to claim a tiny, basic part at the bottom of the chain. Instead, everything has to follow an orderly path, moving its way down through the subsystem and assembly levels first. Taking the extra time to map out these layers step by step with systemic thinking is helpful for project managers and everyone else because it keeps our digital setups clear and stops the overall architecture from turning into a tangled, confusing mess that nobody on the team can understand (been there, done that).

Beyond just looking at the static structure, which is basically how the parts of a system are organized on paper, we can also think about how these pieces talk to each other while the system is actually running. Parts that sit at the exact same level of the organizational hierarchy need clear, reliable paths to interact well with one another. This sideways, or horizontal, communication happens all over the place, such as between different stakeholders, between matching subsystems, or even down at the lowest level between actual individual components.

To keep this from turning into too chaotic a mess (and there’s a lot of terminology here, system doesn’t even sound like a word anymore) we can try to map out clear and obvious interface connection points. For instance, we can set up service-type connections that act like communication lines passing behavioral messages back and forth, or flow-type connections that track how physical things like energy, data, or materials move through the structure. By defining these pathways so clearly, we take what could be a messy pile of parts and turn it into a much cleaner, more manageable digital setup that everyone on the team can easily understand and work with.

Tracking System Evolution Through Life Cycles

It seems easy to forget that systems are not just static, unmoving objects that stay the same once they are built. Instead, they act more like living entities once spun up that constantly grow, change, and evolve over time. Chapter 4 of our course textbook, Systems Engineering Demystified (Holt, 2023), explains that every system has a "life cycle" which maps out how that entity evolves structurally from the very first idea until the day it is completely retired. Feels almost esoteric, like Dr. Frankenstein stitching together not just a man, but a living, breathing system. Anyways. To make this easy to manage (unlike a monster), we break a system's lifespan down into separate blocks of time known as stages. While the life cycle itself shows us the structural pieces of this timeline, we use a "life cycle model" to map out the behavior of the system, defining the step-by-step order in which these different stages are executed.

If you have ever worked on a simple mobile app or website, a standard software development lifecycle might feel pretty familiar, but real-world engineering forces us to look at a much bigger picture. In a complex digital environment, we have to track seven different types of lifecycles running at the exact same time, which includes looking at systems, projects, programs, assets, procurements, products, and technologies. Every single one of these elements undergoes its own unique journey. For example, a core system life cycle moves through six major stages: conception (the initial idea), development (designing the blueprint), production (actually building it), utilization (people using the tool), support (fixing bugs and maintaining it), and retirement (shutting it down safely).

Meanwhile, a procurement life cycle focuses on the business side of buying and acquiring tools, moving through stages like writing the initial concept, figuring out vendor prices, demonstrating capabilities, manufacturing the product, keeping it in-service, and managing final disposal. On top of that, we should monitor a technology life cycle, tracking its research, creation, maturity, and decline, just to make sure the tech we are using does not become obsolete or out-of-date while our system is still out in the world.

Trying to manage how all these different lifelines cross paths can quickly become an overwhelming design puzzle. To handle this complexity without losing our minds, we can try to locate the "interaction points" where these different lifecycles bump into each other, and we chart their paths using clear behavior views. “Behavior view,” by the way, is the practice of looking at a problem as a continuous pattern over time, rather than a series of isolated events. For instance, when a large organization decides to buy a new software tool, they are following their own internal asset life cycle, which has to directly interact and align with the developer’s active development life cycle too.

Moving past any of these major milestones is controlled by what is called in our text “stage gates.” We can think of a stage gate as a strict quality control review meeting where all the stakeholders check the work. A gate forces a project team to evaluate their progress and choose a next step: progress to the next stage if everything is perfect, stay put in the current stage to fix mistakes, go back to a previous stage to do a major redesign, or cancel the entire project lifecycle due to a big failure (woops). Taking the time to map out these evolutionary paths using sequence diagrams helps us see the whole picture, minimizing the hidden gaps and communication breakdowns where real-world tech projects usually stall, run over budget, or fail entirely.

Establishing Consistent and Measurable Processes

The final piece of putting together a great system design involves mastering the exact daily activities and steps that happen along our project timeline. As Chapter 5 points out, a "process" is basically just a structured step-by-step checklist of activities that a team follows to achieve a specific, successful goal. To keep a big engineering project from becoming messy, confusing, or unrealistic, our processes need to have three essential qualities. First, they must be repeatable, meaning anyone on the team can follow the exact same steps every time to get a consistent result. Second, they must be measurable, so we can track data and make sure the team is working efficiently. Lastly, they must be demonstrable, which means we can easily show outside managers or inspectors that our work fully complies with strict industry quality standards. Lots of definitions for short words.

Under an MBSE lens, a process acts kind of like a bridge combining three elements: behavioral activities, the digital or non-digital pieces produced or consumed by those activities, and the stakeholders responsible for executing them. To help design teams navigate this data safely, international standards like ISO 15288 organize these workflows into four logical “process groups.” The first of these is the Technical Process Group, which handles everyday engineering tasks such as business or mission analysis, stakeholder needs definition, system requirements definition, architecture design, implementation, and system verification and validation. Alongside these engineering workflows sits the Technical Management Process Group, which manages active project activities on a project-by-project basis by creating workflows for planning, risk evaluation, configuration tracking, assessment, control, and measurement. On a broader corporate scale, the Organizational Project-Enabling Process Group sets company-wide business guidelines and infrastructure support through life cycle management, human resources, portfolio management, quality management, and asset upkeep/management. Finally, the Agreement Process Group outlines customer-supplier relationships by establishing and monitoring the needed acquisition and supply steps that run the exchange of products or services.

This focus on standardizing our workflows is backed up by Xue et al. (2017), who looked at how massive tech projects often face friction because the systems engineering teams and the project managers are not on the same page. To bridge this gap, the researchers aligned the technical processes of the ISO 15288 engineering standard with the management guidelines of the PMBoK guide. By mapping these two frameworks together, they built a shared, centralized blueprint that lets engineers and managers speak the same language and work better together. This combined model helps different teams collaborate smoothly without stepping on each other's toes, proving that using structured, standard-driven processes is one of the best ways to cut down on human error, align different departments together, and lower operational risks during big digital projects.

To bring these structural definitions to life, we map out a process's physiology using a process behavior view, often drawn as a SysML activity diagram. This model combines actions, objects, and swimlanes to align activities directly with the specific stakeholders responsible for them. By building a centralized repository of process content views and tracing their flows sequentially, we construct a reliable, consistent, and fully auditable blueprint capable of surviving real-world digital complexity.

Wrapping it Up and Reflection

Reflecting on these system concepts, it is clear (and often repeated by now) that professional digital design requires me to look beyond the boundary of isolated pieces. By exploring systems and interfaces, life cycles, and standard-driven process frameworks, I can see how structural choices can impact an entire ecosystem. Both within the system and also external to it. Whether mapping out the physical and abstract boundaries of an expanding architecture, tracking a product's timeline through strict stage gates, or making workflows that align with international benchmarks like ISO 15288, it is all about having a centralized model. Using these integrated model views gives us a way to turn messy real-world requirements of projects into actual realities.

Module 2.1 Blog Post

Shaping Digital Spaces Around Human Behavior and Empathy (Aww)

Designing for people means adopting a philosophy known as, and aptly named, “human-centered design.” This practice places the needs, behaviors, mental habits, and biological limitations of actual people at the focus of each phase of product development. Instead of forcing users to adapt to the logic of a computer system, human-centered design tries to meet human expectations. By studying how the mind processes visual information, how attention shifts under stress, and how past experiences shape present choices, we can build digital spaces that feel a bit more natural, accessible, and welcoming.

Some Different Options: Comparing ISO 9241-210 and IDEO

To put human-centered design into action, organizations rely on structured frameworks that guide their development teams. Two of the most widely recognized frameworks in the industry come from fairly different origins: the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the global design firm IDEO. While both frameworks share the goal of prioritizing human welfare in the creation of their products, they have pretty distinct pathways for achieving that result.

The formal engineering perspective is represented by the standard known as ISO 9241-210. This document outlines an iterative process framework consisting of interconnected steps aimed at better usability and user experience. The ISO standard approaches human-centered design with the discipline of a more traditional engineering workflow. It begins with comprehensive planning, then moves directly into analyzing and specifying the exact context of use. This means developers must clearly outline who will use the system, what tasks they need to accomplish, and what environmental factors will surround them. From there, teams draft testable user requirements that are hopefully free of contradictions. Only after these foundations are set up does the team produce design solutions, such as prototypes, which are then evaluated by real users against the original requirements list. This loop keeps repeating until the design successfully matches every documented user requirement.

In contrast, IDEO’s framework (IDEO.org, 2015) presents a flexible approach driven by specific mindsets. Instead of step-by-step engineering instructions, IDEO groups the design process into three broad phases: Inspiration, Ideation, and Implementation. During the Inspiration phase, designers immerse themselves in the daily lives of communities to try to build personal empathy. In the Ideation phase, teams make sense of their field research by shifting between open-ended brainstorming and more practical prototyping, a pattern known as diverging and converging. Finally, the Implementation phase brings the concept together by mapping out its market delivery and long-term sustainability.

The major differences between these two frameworks is in their underlying execution styles. The ISO standard relies on a formal, documentation-heavy approach where requirements need to be checked off in an auditable way. IDEO, on the other hand, relies heavily on its seven core mindsets, including creative confidence, embracing ambiguity, and learning from failure. IDEO treats prototyping not just as a milestone to verify requirements, but as a quick, tactile way to think and learn through creation. Despite these big structural variations, they still have shared ideas. Both models reject linear approaches where engineers build a whole product in isolation before showing it to users. Both explicitly state that theoretical desk research is not enough and developers should actively involve real users throughout the design loop to gather meaningful feedback.

Human Sight Limits, Perception, and Visual Hierarchy

To design effective screens, we should try to understand the biological limitations of the human visual system. As Dr. Jeff Johnson (2021) explains, many software developers assume that human vision operates like a high-resolution digital camera, evenly capturing details across a layout. Whereas actually, our eyes process visual information in a highly uneven, patchy, and easily tricked manner.

Our sharpest vision is restricted to a tiny central region of the retina called the fovea, which covers only about one percent of our entire visual field. Dr. Johnson (2021) notes that holding your thumb out at arm's length gives a fair approximation of the fovea's reach. Within this itty bitty window, our brain receives a high-resolution, clear image; outside of it, our peripheral vision is remarkably blurry. We enjoy the illusion of a completely sharp visual environment only because our eyes constantly dart around about three times per second, piece by piece, allowing our brain to process that orbital information and patch together a smooth mental picture.

This biological reality has major importance for interface design. If an error message or layout shift happens away from where a user's eyes are directly focused, they will often fail to notice it completely. For example, if a user clicks a "Submit" button at the bottom right of a page, their foveal focus is locked onto that exact coordinate. If an error alert quietly displays in a text bar at the far top left, it happens within their low-resolution peripheral vision. Unless that message uses special properties, like high-contrast boldness or a brief animation, to actively pull the user's attention, it remains an unnoticeable blur.

Additionally, human color vision is constrained. Our brains process color by subtracting signals from our retinal cone cells, routing information through color-opponent channels. Because of this subtractive architecture, our eyes are naturally optimized to spot edge contrast and rapid light changes rather than absolute brightness levels. Our ability to tell colors apart drops rapidly if those colors are pale, if the colored elements are tiny, or if they are separated by wide screen distances. Design configurations that rely on color alone to communicate status will inevitably isolate individuals with color vision deficiencies and lead to operational mistakes.

To overcome these biological blind spots, designers use the principles of visual hierarchy and Gestalt psychology. As Dr. Johnson (2021) demonstrates, Gestalt principles show that our visual system automatically groups nearby objects together (Proximity) and treats similar-looking shapes as part of a single pattern (Similarity). By using variations in size, contrast, spacing, and placement, designers can construct a clearer path that guides a user's eyes through a layout in order of importance.

When headings are large and bold, they better stand out in our peripheral vision, telling our brain right where to send our eyes next and lowering the amount of unnecessary time and fixations spent on a page (Nielsen et Pernice, 2010, p. 77). When information is broken into clean visual blocks with ample white space, our brain more easily separates different topics without as much cognitive exhaustion.

Interface Architecture and the Four Laws of UX

To manage these competing workflows, user experience researchers rely on interaction guidelines known as the Laws of UX, compiled by Jon Yablonski (2020). These laws serve as guidelines to shape software architecture to match how our brains naturally process data, recall information, and make decisions.

Jakob’s Law: Using Familiar Patterns

Jakob’s Law states that users spend most of their time on other digital products, meaning they expect your website or application to behave much like the ones they already know. Human beings naturally build mental frames, or expectations, based on their long-term digital habits. When they navigate to a new online store, they expect to see a shopping cart icon in the top right corner and a familiar few-step checkout sequence. By matching these deep-seated mental models, we can allow users to focus on their goals rather than wasting time learning a new layout.

Fitts’s Law: Target Sizes and Trajectories

Fitts’s Law states that the time required to swiftly move to and acquire a target depends on the target's physical size and its overall distance from the user's starting position. In touch interfaces or desktop displays, interactive elements like primary action buttons should be large enough to be hit accurately without accidental miss-clicks. They also need to be spaced far enough apart to prevent users from accidentally triggering adjacent, unwanted actions.

Miller’s Law: Helping Working Memory

Miller’s Law explains that the average person can keep only about seven, plus or minus two, distinct bits of information in their active working memory at any given moment. Our short-term cognitive capacity is highly limited and easily overwhelmed. To prevent mental exhaustion, interfaces should break large blocks of content into smaller, digestible visual groups, a technique widely known as “data chunking.” We see this law in action every day with credit card fields and phone numbers, where separating strings into spaced blocks makes them easier for our short-term memory to verify.

Hick’s Law: Streamlining Choices

Finally, Hick’s Law states that the time it takes to make a decision scales with the overall number and complexity of choices available. If a user lands on a screen and is hit with lots of equally prominent buttons and text alerts, their brain experiences decision paralysis. For more complicated processes, Hick's Law recommends breaking the bigger tasks into smaller, sequential steps, minimizing decision drag (Proctor et al., 2018). Highlighting a single recommended pathway or pre-selecting smart defaults/recommendations prevents users from feeling too overwhelmed by options.

Conclusion

Designing for people does not seem like just a simple matter of choosing attractive color palettes or arranging sleek layouts. It requires an intentional, continuous effort to align technological structures with the natural constraints of human biology and psychology. Running my designs through structured engineering standards or IDEO’s iterative phases is a great way to smooth out rough spots early on, making sure the final product actually feels natural to use. Whether I follow traditional ISO workflows or IDEO's empathetic mindsets, the goal is the same: lessen systemic issues before the software goes live. It was enjoyable to step back and look at the big picture for this assignment, and the frameworks will definitely help me look at software applications with human biology and psychology in mind in the future.

How Memory, Habits, and Complexity Shape User Experience

We’ve all dealt with apps that feel like a chore to navigate. This frustration rarely happens because of just bad code or bad graphics. It can happen because the system forces us to fight our biological wiring, draining our mental energy. True usability means designing digital environments with how our brains naturally process data, form habits, and apply mental shortcuts. Because human working memory is limited and our attention is incredibly selective, filtering out our visual surroundings based on our immediate goals. Designers must do the heavy lifting early on so software treats the human mind as a delicate asset rather than an infinite storage bin.

Intro to Memory, Habits, and Mental Models

"The Working Memory Model" by UX Stalin is licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0

Our active working memory isn't a static storage container either; it is the combined focus of our attention at any given moment. Pretty cool actually. Historically, early interface guidelines suggested that humans could keep roughly seven pieces of information in our immediate awareness. Modern empirical studies show that working memory capacity is dependent on additional variables, but closer to only three to five distinct elements at a time (Cowan, 2012). This limited capacity means our attention is highly focused and selective, filtering out most of our visual surroundings based entirely on our immediate goals.

Because immediate awareness hits a ceiling so quickly, our brains rely on mental shortcuts called mental models. These are internal collections of expectations built from repetitive past experiences. When a person opens a new website, they bring an internal roadmap forged by sometimes thousands of hours spent navigating other software platforms. They expect search fields to sit near the top, settings to reside within a gear symbol, and confirmation buttons to follow uniform arrangements.

When software respects these expectations, interaction occurs with minimal mental drag. Users fall into comfortable, automatic habits, freeing up working memory to focus on their primary task instead of solving puzzles just to figure out how the layout operates. However, when an interface unexpectedly breaks these patterns, like flipping the usual left-right order of confirmation/cancellation or Yes/No buttons, it disrupts this natural experience. The user’s brain drops out of its faster, automatic processing mode and is forced into slow, conscious problem-solving mode. This sudden shift also causes a spike in mental fatigue and increases mistakes.

Interface Consistency and Feedback

Consistency across digital environments helps memory overload. When functions, phrases, and visual layouts operate at least similarly across a platform, the total amount of new information a user must memorize drops significantly. Inconsistent software requires individuals to commit multiple unique context rules to their long-term memory, increasing the chance that their brain will misremember a procedure, lose track of a setting, or make a severe operational error. Consistency allows users to confidently apply what they learned on their very first screen to every subsequent page, drastically flattening the learning curve.

Alongside consistency, system feedback keeps human actions on track. Human behavior relies on a continuous cyclical pattern known as the human action cycle. In this loop, an individual forms a specific goal, breaks that goal down into tasks, executes those actions on a device, and then evaluates the screen to see if their action worked. Without clear, instant feedback, the evaluation phase of this cycle falls apart. Users are left wondering if the computer registered their click, if a network request is loading, or if the system experienced a complete crash. Usable systems prevent this confusion by supplying continuous feedback, such as explicit progress bars and clear status changes, letting users verify their progress without guesswork.

How Poor Layout Choices Taxes our Minds

When an interface ignores these cognitive constraints, trouble shows up as an increase in cognitive load. One major design flaw occurs when teams misunderstand the core difference between recognition and recall. Recognition happens when a person sees a visual element or menu choice that matches an experience, instantly reviving a neural path with minimal effort. Recall, on the other hand, requires the brain to fish for information or commands out of long-term memory without immediate visual help, a process that is slower and error-prone.

Modern graphical interfaces solved this issue by shifting to a “see-and-choose” model, displaying clearer options and icons that allow human recognition to do the work. Unfortunately, many modern applications still slip into recall-heavy traps, such as during security registration sequences. When an application forces users to select from a set list of preset security questions, like asking for the city they were born in or the name of their first pet, it creates a memory burden. Sometimes the way these questions are phrased individuals do not have a single, perfect answer to them, forcing users to write down their responses or risk account lockouts when they fail to recall the exact text string years later.

Another source of unnecessary mental load stems from a failure to recognize that reading is a unique, complex human skill. While human brains are biologically hardwired to acquire spoken language naturally, our visual processing systems are systematically trained during childhood to decode written text. When a person reads, their eyes execute rapid jumps called saccades, pausing briefly on central words via eye fixations to parse shapes and letters. Our eyes also process this text accurately only within the small foveal spotlight at the center of our gaze.

"A Hard Read" by Moise Nicu is licensed under CC BY 2.0

Designers drop users out of fast, automatic reading modes when they implement poor layout properties, such as printing body text in all capital letters, which does slow reading speed due to a lack of visual practice. Centering long, multi-line paragraphs of body text acts as a massive speed bump. Because every centered line starts at a different horizontal coordinate, the brain cannot accurately predict where the next line begins, forcing the user to consciously adjust their gaze at the start of every single row.

More on Interface Complexity and Tesler’s Law

To build digital environments that prevent these issues, engineering teams lean on foundational behavioral laws like Tesler’s Law. This law states that every system has a baseline of inherent complexity that cannot be designed away. In usable systems, engineers do the heavy lifting behind the scenes, writing background development architecture to absorb this difficulty and spare the user from unnecessary steps. However, designers must be careful not to over-simplify the interface to the point of complete abstraction, where the meaning of icons is entirely lost.

"London (35 of 87)" by Betsy Weber is licensed under CC BY 2.0

This balancing act ties directly to a major idea from cognitive text research: human perception is deeply filtered by our active, immediate goals. When navigating an interface, people do not read screens carefully like when they are reading an open novel. They aggressively scan layout components looking for labels that match their active objective (Vdovenko, 2023). This strategy means individuals follow a strict information pattern, interpreting interface labels at face value and hunting for what they are looking for.

The second principle that shapes layout usability is our reliance on external memory aids to keep track of our actions. Because human working memory is highly volatile, our attention is pulled away from past details soon after we move to a new task. Once a user accomplishes their primary goal, the details of that workflow quickly drop out of their immediate awareness. This vulnerability often leads to closure slips, where users finish a main action but completely forget secondary cleanup steps, such as leaving an open banking portal logged in on a public device. More and more systems counter this vulnerability by turning the interface into an external memory board, using visual changes to indicate completed phases, and using automated background triggers to sign out open accounts or auto-save unsaved files.

Structuring the Collaborative Engineering Workflow

To transition these psychological rules from abstract textbooks into everyday team workflows, software organizations establish operational design principles. Design principles do not exist as vague, obvious phrases like "our interface should be user-friendly." Instead, as emphasized by the best practices detailed in Laws of UX, useful team guidelines should take a firm stance, resolve real structural questions, and establish explicit engineering trade-offs (Yablonski, 2020, pp. 40-41). They can act as a shared collective vocabulary that helps team members to say no to distracting features and maintain a unified architectural vision.

To build a reliable design framework, a team can run cross-functional workshops to tie project goals to established behavioral laws. For instance, as mapped out step-by-step in Laws of UX, a team might establish a guiding principle like "Familiarity over novelty." To fulfill this goal, the framework requires mapping the target objective to an observation of human psychology, like Jakob's Law, which notes that users expect your system to work like other sites they know. This observation is then converted into actionable rules for team members, such as utilizing common design patterns or avoiding distracting, flashy UI animations. This collaborative process creates a roadmap that makes sure daily interface choices are grounded in verified human psychology rather than random personal preferences of designers.

Conclusion

Usability is achieved through a continuous alignment of technology with the biological reality of human nature, meeting users halfway by respecting visual limitations, cognitive patterns, and memory quirks. By evaluating platforms through structured process frameworks or empathy-driven pathways, teams can dismantle system barriers hopefully before a public release. As software ecosystems scale in complexity, engineering responsibilities should try to remain grounded in basic human behavior, acknowledging our little foveal focus fields, short-term working memory capacity, and vulnerability to choice paralysis. Utilizing Tesler’s Law to handle internal complexity behind the scenes and structuring digital environments around scannability helps the user's finite mental energy, and allows us to craft digital environments that feel more natural to use.
